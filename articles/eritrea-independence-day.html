35 Years of Eritrea — Why Every African Independence Story Is My Story Too

On the 35th anniversary of Eritrean independence, a reflection on what sovereignty means for this generation of African founders — and why the next independence won't be political.

CI
Chaste Inegbedion Editor-in-Chief
May 24, 2026 · 4 min read

Thirty-five years ago today, Eritrea became the youngest nation on the African continent.

After three decades of war — first against Italian colonialism, then against Ethiopian annexation — the Eritrean people voted overwhelmingly for sovereignty in a 1993 referendum. It wasn't given. It was taken. And that distinction matters more than most people realise.

I'm Nigerian. I didn't grow up hearing Eritrean stories in my history classes. Most of us didn't. The African continent has 54 nations, each with its own origin story of sovereignty, and yet we are raised knowing maybe three or four of them well. That's the first problem.

The second problem is that we keep telling independence stories as if they ended.

Independence Is Not a Date. It's a Practice.

When we celebrate May 24 in Asmara, or October 1 in Lagos, or March 6 in Accra, we are not celebrating a finish line. We are marking the starting gun of a race that never ends — a race to build something that actually works for the people who fought to have it.

Eritrea's story is instructive here. The country won its independence through one of the longest liberation wars in modern African history. And then it faced the harder battle: building institutions, creating economic infrastructure, and navigating a world that wasn't designed to make room for small, newly sovereign African states.

That is a story every African founder I know is living right now.

The Next Independence Won't Be Political

I spend most of my time in rooms where African entrepreneurs are building technology companies — fintech platforms, AI tools, health systems, media ventures. And what I hear, over and over, is a generation that understands independence differently.

They're not fighting for a flag. They're fighting for infrastructure.

Consider: Africa has 1.4 billion people and produces less than 2% of global AI research. Our payment rails are still controlled by foreign intermediaries who take 3–7% of every cross-border transaction. Our stories are still told, disproportionately, by outlets headquartered in New York and London.

The next wave of African independence will not be won at the ballot box. It will be won in data centres, in cap tables, in editorial rooms, and in the regulatory frameworks that determine who gets to build — and who gets to benefit from what's built.

One Africa

When Eritrea gained independence, some people saw it as a loss — a fractured Ethiopia, a smaller Horn. But sovereignty isn't about size. It's about self-determination. The right to build something on your own terms, even when the world thinks you're too small, too young, or too risky to succeed.

That's the same bet every African founder is making.

I've sat across from policymakers at the World Bank who treat Africa as a single problem set. I've been in rooms at the UN where 54 distinct nations are compressed into a single panel. And I've watched some of the brightest entrepreneurs on the continent build in spite of systems that were never designed to support them.

What Eritrea teaches us — what every African independence story teaches us — is that sovereignty is not a gift. It is an act of construction. It is the daily choice to build something that reflects who you are, not who the world expects you to be.

Today, I celebrate Eritrea. But I'm also celebrating Nairobi's tech corridor, Lagos's creator economy, Kigali's innovation city, and every African building something worth owning.

One continent. Fifty-four independence stories. One ongoing act of construction.

Happy Independence Day, Eritrea. The rest of us are still building too.
