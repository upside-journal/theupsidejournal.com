Key Takeaways

  • A record cohort of African tech founders will attend Cannes Lions 2026, reflecting the continent's growing influence in global creative technology
  • African startups raised $2.4B in 2025 — and Cannes is becoming a critical pipeline for international partnerships
  • The Pitch Journey programme, supported by The Upside Journal, is helping founders navigate the Cannes ecosystem strategically
  • From fintech to creator tools to event tech, African founders are solving problems the global market hasn't even named yet

There is a photograph from Cannes Lions 2019 that circulates in African tech WhatsApp groups every June. It shows the main stage of the Palais des Festivals — 12,000 seats, a curved LED wall the size of a tennis court, and a speaker lineup featuring 47 names. Not one is African.

That image is about to become a historical artefact.

Cannes Lions 2026 will host the largest contingent of African tech founders in the festival's 73-year history. They are coming not as tourists, not as spectators, but as operators — founders running companies that are solving problems the Western market is only beginning to understand.

The Founders You Need to Know

The Fintech Operator: Building Cross-Border Rails

One founder arriving from Lagos is running a fintech platform that processes cross-border payments between African markets and European brands. The company handles over $40 million in monthly transaction volume and counts three Cannes Lions sponsors among its clients.

At Cannes, this founder isn't looking for press coverage. They are closing a distribution partnership with a European payment processor that would triple their corridor capacity. The meeting was arranged three months ago through a shared contact at a Semaform Foundation convening.

The Creator-Tool Builder: Nairobi's Answer to Canva

From Nairobi comes a founder whose design platform has accumulated 1.2 million users across East Africa. The tool is purpose-built for mobile-first markets — optimised for low-bandwidth connections, available in Swahili and Amharic, and priced at a fraction of Western alternatives.

The Cannes agenda: meetings with two agency holding companies exploring white-label licensing for African market campaigns. If the deal closes, it would represent the first time a Nairobi-built creative tool powers campaigns for global brands at scale.

The Event-Tech Innovator: Cape Town to the Croisette

A Cape Town-based startup has built a platform for live event analytics — tracking foot traffic, engagement patterns, and attendee sentiment in real time. The technology was developed for South African music festivals and has since been deployed at corporate events across the continent.

Cannes Lions is the proving ground: a pilot deployment at a sponsor activation will test whether the platform can handle the density and complexity of a tier-one global event. If successful, the founder has a letter of intent from a major events company.

Why Cannes Matters for African Tech

The conventional wisdom is that African founders go to Cannes for visibility. The reality is more strategic than that.

Access to capital corridors: European and American VCs who would never fly to Lagos for a pitch meeting will take a 20-minute coffee at the Carlton Hotel. The cost-per-introduction at Cannes is dramatically lower than any other channel for cross-continental fundraising.

Partnership density: In five days, a founder can meet potential distribution partners, technology integrators, and enterprise clients who would otherwise require six months of email outreach. The compressed timeline forces decision-making.

Credibility transfer: Being at Cannes — especially in a curated context like The Upside Journal's Pitch Journey — signals a level of seriousness and readiness that opens doors. It is not fair, but it is real: a founder who has "been to Cannes" is perceived differently in subsequent fundraising conversations.

The Pitch Journey Model

The Upside Journal's Pitch Journey programme exists because Cannes without context is just expensive tourism.

The programme provides:

  • Curated introductions to decision-makers mapped to each founder's specific deal objectives
  • Content amplification through Upside's editorial platform, giving founders a credible media presence during the festival
  • Relationship intelligence via ConcordeApp, which maps the founder's target network and identifies warm introduction paths

The goal is not to "showcase African tech" as a feel-good narrative. It is to create measurable outcomes: meetings booked, partnerships initiated, and term sheets advanced.

The Bigger Picture: Africa's Creative Economy Moment

The numbers tell a story of acceleration. African startups raised $2.4 billion in 2025, down from the 2022 peak of $6.5 billion but more concentrated in revenue-generating companies. The creative economy — encompassing ad tech, content platforms, creator tools, and event technology — represents a growing share.

Cannes Lions sits at the intersection of the two industries where Africa has the most to offer and the most to gain: creative services and technology. The founders heading to the Croisette this month are not making a symbolic gesture. They are executing a strategy.

And for the first time, they have the infrastructure — editorial platforms like The Upside Journal, relationship tools like ConcordeApp, and convening networks like the Semaform Foundation — to convert that strategy into results.
