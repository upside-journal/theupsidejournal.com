Every booth on the Croisette has an AI demo. Most of them are a screen, a prompt box, and a prayer.

Let's be honest about what's happening at Cannes Lions 2026: the AI hype cycle has reached the point where not having an AI demo at your activation is like not having Wi-Fi. It's table stakes. And like Wi-Fi, most of it barely works under load.

But buried among the vapourware and the "powered by AI" stickers slapped on tools that were perfectly functional without it, there are a handful of demonstrations that represent genuine capability leaps. These are the ones worth your time.

Category 2: Audience Intelligence

The second category is less flashy but potentially more valuable: AI tools that help media companies and brands understand their audience in real time.

[INSERT: Specific tool/demo] — [Description]

Traditional audience analytics operate on a 24–72 hour delay. At Cannes, where content velocity determines who captures the audience, that delay is a competitive disadvantage. The AI tools in this category promise real-time audience intelligence: who is reading what, which topics are gaining traction, and where the engagement gaps are.

The question is whether the intelligence is actionable. A dashboard that tells you "your article on AI ethics is trending" is useless if it arrives after three competitors have already published follow-ups. The tools that win will be the ones that pair observation with recommendation: "This topic is trending. Here's a headline. Here's the optimal distribution channel. Publish now."

Category 3: Relationship and Event Intelligence

This is the category that matters most — and the one where ConcordeApp is making its Cannes debut.

Relationship intelligence at events is a fundamentally different problem from content creation or audience analytics. It involves mapping unstructured human interactions — conversations in hallways, introductions at dinners, follow-up texts sent at midnight — into a structured graph that reveals patterns.

ConcordeApp's live deployment at Cannes Lions 2026 is testing whether this can work at scale. The platform is tracking:

  • Interaction density across key venues (which locations generate the most follow-up actions)
  • Network clustering (which groups of people are repeatedly converging)
  • Deal velocity (the time between first introduction and first formal meeting)

The Vapourware Red Flags

Not every AI demo at Cannes deserves attention. Three red flags to watch for:

1. "Just imagine…" language: If the demo starts with a hypothetical instead of a live output, walk away 2. Pre-recorded demonstrations: If the AI output was generated before you arrived, you're watching a video, not a demo 3. No pricing discussion: If the company won't tell you what it costs, it doesn't have a product — it has a pitch deck

What Matters Beyond the Demo

The AI tools that will actually reshape creative work are not the ones with the flashiest Cannes booth. They are the ones that will be in daily use six months from now.

Ask three questions:

  • Who is already paying for this?
  • What does the workflow look like on a Tuesday morning, not a Cannes stage?
  • Does this make my team faster, or does it just make my demo reel prettier?
